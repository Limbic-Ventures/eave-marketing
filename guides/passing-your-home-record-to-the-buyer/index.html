What conveys with the house: passing on your home's record

You already list the fridge and the washer. Your home's maintenance history can convey too — and it's worth more than the appliances.

What "conveys" means

In a real-estate contract, "conveys" is the word for what stays with the house when it sells. The dishwasher usually conveys; the patio furniture usually doesn't. It's a line every buyer and seller negotiates without thinking much about it — the physical stuff that transfers at closing.

But the most valuable thing a well-kept home can hand its next owner isn't physical at all. It's the knowledge: what's been fixed, what's been replaced, how old the roof really is, which plumber actually shows up. That information almost never conveys — it walks out the door with the seller — and the new owner spends their first two years rebuilding it from scratch, usually the expensive way.

Smart-home gear and records

Modern homes have made this sharper. A house today comes with a thermostat that has an account, a doorbell camera with a subscription, a solar inverter with a warranty, a water heater with a registered serial number. When these change hands, the hardware conveys but the records rarely do — so the buyer inherits a smart home they can't fully log into and can't prove the age of.

Gathering the model numbers, install dates, warranties, and manuals into one place — and handing that over — turns "here are the gadgets" into "here's everything you need to run them." It's a small act that saves the next owner months of guessing.

How a home's memory transfers

This is exactly what a home knowledge base is for. Everything you've captured while you lived there — projects, materials, contractors, appliance specs, service history, documents — is already organized by room and system. At closing, that record can transfer with the sale, so the new owner starts with the full history instead of a blank slate. They walk in on day one already knowing the furnace filter size and the last time the septic was pumped.

Because it's your record, you decide what transfers and when — the hand-off happens with your consent, as part of the sale. See transferring your home to a new owner for how it works and what carries over.

Why a documented home negotiates better

There's a self-interested reason to do this too, and it shows up before closing. A home that arrives with a complete, organized record reads as a home that was cared for — and cared-for homes give buyers less to worry about and less to negotiate against. When the inspection turns up an aging water heater and you can show it was serviced every year with the receipts to prove it, that's not a price chip anymore; it's reassurance. Documentation is quiet leverage. It's the difference between "we're not sure how this place was maintained" and "here's the proof."
